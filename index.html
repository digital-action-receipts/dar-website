Customer-owned evidence

A receipt is the authority — not the vendor database.

Digital Action Receipts (DAR) are signed, tamper-evident evidence artifacts emitted after digital actions commit. They are stored in customer-controlled immutable storage and can be independently verified later for audits, disputes, investigations, and post-hoc accountability review.

A DAR proves occurrence — not correctness, intent, authorization, or compliance. DAR follows a principle of receipt neutrality: it asserts that an action occurred, not whether it should have occurred.

What DAR is

  • Evidence of what happened, when it happened, and the actor/system involved (as asserted at action time)
  • Issued at action time (not reconstructed later)
  • Customer-custodied (written to customer-controlled storage)
  • Independently verifiable (cryptographic integrity can be checked outside the issuing system)
  • Minimal by design (references context; does not default to storing sensitive payloads)
post-commit append-only customer custody verifiable neutral
DAR is not monitoring. DAR is not decision logic. DAR is not enforcement. DAR is not a system of record.

Where the receipts live

Receipts are written into customer-controlled immutable storage. This preserves custody and avoids a critical dispute failure mode: the vendor controlling the authoritative evidence.

Examples of common customer-controlled immutable storage options include:

  • AWS S3 Object Lock
  • Azure Immutable Blob
  • GCP Retention Bucket
DAR may provide issuance and verification tooling, but does not become the owner of your evidence.

Why now

Systems are increasingly automated and agent-driven. As autonomous systems act with less human intervention, the demand stays the same: “Prove what happened under which conditions.” DAR makes that proof portable.

What a receipt preserves

A receipt anchors the action and the referenced context that matters for later review: policy/version references, model or configuration identifiers, stable correlation, and integrity proof—without copying private data.

What DAR avoids

DAR avoids becoming surveillance, a data lake, or a system of enforcement. The goal is defensible evidence with minimal retained data and clear custody boundaries.

How to think about it

If a vendor system disappears, changes, or is disputed, the receipt remains valid. Like a paper receipt: the database is a convenience; the receipt is the authority.

  • Action happens → an evidence artifact is emitted after the action commits
  • Evidence references are stored → in customer-controlled immutable storage
  • Later verification → receipts can be checked for integrity and rendered as a human-readable timeline for review

Risk note: issuing DARs does not require broad disclosure. Receipts can be shared selectively under existing legal, policy, and access controls.

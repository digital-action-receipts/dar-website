Verifiable receipts for digital actions.

Digital Action Receipts (DARs) are issued at the moment an action occurs and preserved as append-only, tamper-evident records. They provide a durable reference to what happened — before disputes, audits, incident reviews, or narrative reconstruction.

DAR (core definition)
DAR = a verifiable, append-only record of a digital action, issued at execution time. It preserves: actor · action · object · timestamp · reference · policy/context (optional) · integrity proof DARs are designed to remain valid even if the originating system changes, fails, or is disputed.
disputes audits incidents

When systems are questioned later, mutable logs and narratives fail. DAR provides a stable reference that survives remediation and hindsight. DAR replaces retrospective reconstruction with contemporaneous proof.

What this is (and isn’t)

DAR is an evidentiary layer: it preserves facts. It does not interpret, score, or enforce.

  • Append-only: no rewriting history.
  • Tamper-evident: integrity proof travels with the receipt.
  • Verifiable: confirm authenticity by reference.
  • Customer custody: receipts live in customer-controlled storage.
Authority boundary: DAR does not participate in execution, control flow, or decision-making. It establishes authority over evidence of action — nothing more, nothing less.

How DAR fits into existing systems

DAR is an evidentiary layer — not an execution, control, or decision layer. Engineers add DAR with minimal coupling: issue a receipt where an action is committed; store it append-only; verify it later by reference. DAR complements logs and observability by preserving an immutable anchor for the most consequential actions.

  • Write-heavy, read-light: optimized for issuance at scale and verification on demand.
  • No runtime ML inference: receipts are evidence artifacts, not predictions.
  • Neutral boundary: provides proof of action without becoming the arbiter of disputes.
  • Independent authority: receipts remain verifiable without relying on mutable logs, dashboards, or narratives.
